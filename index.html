SANSKRIT

Sanskrit is a classical language within the Indo-Aryan branch of Indo-European languages. Emerging in South Asia after the Bronze Age, it played a pivotal role as the sacred language of Hinduism, classical Hindu philosophy, and historical texts of Buddhism and Jainism. Serving as a link language in ancient South Asia, it spread with Hindu and Buddhist cultures to Southeast Asia, East Asia, and Central Asia, influencing formal vocabularies.

Sanskrit encompasses various Old Indo-Aryan language varieties, with Vedic Sanskrit being the oldest found in the Rigveda (1500 BCE - 1200 BCE). Classical Sanskrit, a refined form codified by Pāṇini in the 1st millennium BCE, marked a significant development. Notable works like the Mahābhārata and Rāmāyaṇa were composed in Epic Sanskrit between 400 BCE and 300 CE. Over time, Sanskrit became tradition-bound, ceased as a first language, and stopped evolving as a living language.

Nomenclature

Nomenclature

1. The term "Sanskrit" is derived from the compound word sáṃskṛta-, where sáṃ means 'together, good, well, perfected,' and kṛta- means 'made, formed, work.' This conveys the idea of a work that is "well prepared, pure, perfect, and sacred," emphasizing tonal qualities over semantics.

2. In ancient India, sound and oral transmission held immense value, leading sages to refine the alphabet, word structure, and grammar into what they called Sanskrit—a "sublime musical mold." The late Vedic period witnessed a fascination with resonating sound and its musical foundations, permeating linguistic, philosophical, and religious literature.

3. Sanskrit competed with vernacular Prakritic languages, termed prākṛta-, meaning "original, natural, normal, artless." Patañjali recognized Prakrit as the instinctive first language, acknowledging its imperfections. Sanskrit, seen as purifying, resolved linguistic issues. Early grammarians like Daṇḍin noted that Prakrit retained Sanskrit roots but suffered from "loss of sounds" and grammatical neglect. Despite differences, both languages coexisted, with scholars like Namisādhu viewing Prakrit as natural and Sanskrit as a refined form through "purification by grammar."

History

Sanskrit, a member of the Indo-European family of languages, is among the earliest ancient documented languages originating from the common root language known as Proto-Indo-European. The three earliest documented languages from this lineage include:


  1. 1. Vedic Sanskrit (c. 1500-500 BCE): - The oldest form, found in the Rigveda, composed between 1500 BCE and 1200 BCE.
  2. 2. Mycenaean Greek (c. 1450 BCE) and Ancient Greek (c. 750-400 BCE): - Greek languages with roots dating back to the Mycenaean period.
  3. 3. Hittite (c. 1750-1200 BCE): - An ancient language of Anatolia (modern-day Turkey).

Other Indo-European languages distantly related to Sanskrit include:


  1. -> Archaic and Classical Latin (c. 600 BCE-100 CE, Italic languages).
  2. -> Gothic (archaic Germanic language, c. 350 CE).
  3. -> Old Norse (c. 200 CE and after).
  4. -> Old Avestan (c. late 2nd millennium BCE) and Younger Avestan (c. 900 BCE).

The closest ancient relatives of Vedic Sanskrit within the Indo-European languages are the Nuristani languages in the Hindu Kush region and the Iranian languages Avestan and Old Persian. Sanskrit belongs to the satem group of the Indo-European languages.


During the colonial era, scholars acquainted with Latin and Greek noticed striking resemblances between Sanskrit and classical European languages, both in vocabulary and grammar. This linguistic connection further reinforces the shared historical roots of these languages.

Influence

Influence

The historical presence of the Sanskrit language extends far beyond South Asia. Inscriptions and literary evidence indicate that Sanskrit was adopted in Southeast Asia and Central Asia during the 1st millennium CE, facilitated by monks, religious pilgrims, and merchants.

Geographic Spread:

South Asia: The region with the largest collection of ancient Sanskrit manuscripts and inscriptions, spanning beyond ancient India.

Southeast Asia and Central Asia: Evidence of Sanskrit adoption in areas such as China (especially Tibetan monasteries), Myanmar, Indonesia, Cambodia, Laos, Vietnam, Thailand, and Malaysia.

Global Discoveries:

Dry High Deserts and Mountains: Sanskrit inscriptions and manuscripts discovered in Nepal, Tibet, Afghanistan, Mongolia, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Tajikistan, and Kazakhstan.

Far East: Sanskrit texts and inscriptions found in Korea and Japan.

Official Status:

In India, Sanskrit is one of the 22 official languages according to the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution.

In 2010, Uttarakhand became the first Indian state to declare Sanskrit as its second official language.

In 2019, Himachal Pradesh followed suit, making Sanskrit its second official language and becoming the second state in India to do so.

Writing

The early history of writing Sanskrit and other languages in ancient India poses challenges, despite extensive scholarly efforts. The earliest possible script, from the Indus Valley civilization (3rd/2nd millennium BCE), remains undeciphered. If any scripts existed in the Vedic period, they have not been preserved. Scholars generally agree that Sanskrit was spoken in an oral society, and an oral tradition preserved the extensive Vedic and Classical Sanskrit literature.

Scholarly Debates:

- Some scholars argue for an oral society basis, citing inconsistencies in transmitted versions of literature from various cultures. They contend that the vastness and consistency of Vedic literature make it improbable to have been solely composed and transmitted orally across generations without being written down.

Lipi and Early Mentions:

- Lipi, meaning "writing, letters, alphabet" in Sanskrit, contextually refers to scripts or any manner of writing. Pāṇini's Astadhyayi, from the 5th or 4th century BCE, mentions lipi in the context of a writing script and education system but does not specify the script.

Buddhist and Jaina Texts:

Early Buddhist and Jaina texts list numerous writing scripts in ancient India, but the historical value of these lists is questioned. The empirical evidence of writing systems for Sanskrit or Prakrit inscriptions before the 3rd century BCE is lacking.

Challenges in Explaining Cultural Complexity:

- The absence of evidence for writing systems before the 3rd century BCE raises questions about the evidently high level of political organization and cultural complexity in ancient India. The difficulty lies in explaining these aspects without a writing system for Sanskrit and other languages.

Datable Writing Systems:

- The oldest datable writing systems for Sanskrit include the Brāhmī script, related Kharoṣṭhī script, and Brahmi derivatives. The Brāhmī script later evolved into various Indic scripts and Southeast Asian scripts.

Ashoka's Inscriptions:

- The rock edicts and pillar inscriptions of the 3rd century BCE Mauryan emperor Ashoka, though not in Sanskrit, represent extensive surviving inscriptions from this period.

Modern Era

Sanskrit holds a sacred and integral role in various religious traditions, including Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism:

Hindu Tradition:

- Worship in Temples: Sanskrit is used during worship in Hindu temples, playing a central role in rituals and ceremonies.

- Rituals and Rites-of-Passage: Hindu rituals, such as weddings, naming ceremonies, and cremations, involve the chanting of Sanskrit hymns. Major festivals like Durga Puja ritually recite entire Sanskrit texts like the Devi Mahatmya.

Buddhist Tradition:

- Newar Buddhism: Sanskrit is used in all monasteries of Newar Buddhism.

- Mahayana and Tibetan Buddhism: Religious texts and sutras in Mahayana and Tibetan Buddhism are composed in Sanskrit, along with vernacular languages.

Jain Tradition:

- Jain Texts: Revered Jain texts, including the Tattvartha Sutra, Ratnakaranda Śrāvakācāra, the Bhaktamara Stotra, and later versions of the Agamas, are written in Sanskrit.

- Mantras and Ritual Language: Sanskrit mantras and the use of Sanskrit as a ritual language have been commonplace among Jains throughout their medieval history.

General Usage:

- Rituals and Rites-of-Passage in Hinduism: Many Hindu rituals, like weddings and naming ceremonies, involve the chanting of Sanskrit hymns. Major Hindu festivals often include the recitation of entire Sanskrit texts.

- Public Recitations: The Bhagavad Gita, a significant Sanskrit text, is recited in diverse settings, ranging from private household readings to public discourses in halls and auditoriums across India and beyond.
